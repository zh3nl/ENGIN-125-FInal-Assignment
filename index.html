Introduction

Artificial Intelligence, otherwise known as AI, is technologies that enable computers to simulate human learning, problem solving, and creativity. As defined by Stryker et al. in their article for IBM, Applications equipped with AI can performed many daily tasks such as answering basic questions, making recommendations to users, and generate different products such as articles, images, and even full-stack websites [1]. The most popular model, which include the infamous ChatGPT, can even create complex and original content in response to a user's prompt.

How is AI created?

In general, generative AI can be simplified into three key phases:

  • Training: Creating a foundational model that represents the data it was trained on. The most popular foundation models today are Large Language Models (LLMs)
  • Tuning: Adapting the model to a specific application, either through fine tuning with situation-specific data and prompts or through Reinforcement Learning with Human Feedback (RLHF), where humans evaluate the accuracy of model outputs
  • Generation and Evaluation: Applications with generative AI is regularly assessed and tuned to create more relevant responses. Retrieval Augemented Generation is also used to extend the foundational model to out-of-scope data that are deemed relevant for training
Use Cases and Ethical Dilemmas

With AI becoming more efficient and more accurate, it offers versatile benefits across various industries:

  • Software Engineering: Provide code snippet suggests, debug code errors, and create small-scale applications
  • Data Science: Automate data collection, preprocessing, and generate basic analysis
  • Business: Automate customer service (chatbots), provide manufacturing management, and assist with job recruitment
  • Healthcase: Automate telemedicine and provide basic diagnosis
  • Education: Generate lecture materials for teachers and study materials for students

Because of its immense potential in automation and scaling, many companies and organizations are scrambling to capitalize on the latest AI technologies. As a result, it is important to address and adapt to the many risks and ethical dilemmas posed by the use of AI as it becomes increasingly influential in our lives. Because of its novel status, many applications of AI remains in the gray area of legal and ethical standards, making it very difficult to assess its impact to humanity. While AI can significantly improve the human workflow, it also comes with several concerns, particularly in the following issues:

  • Potential data breaches that violate individual privacy
  • The presence of bias within Large Language Models, particularly against traditionally underrepresented minority groups
  • The replacement of human decision-making with AI, even though mainstream LLM research ignores the psychological diversity of people around the world
  • The lack of transparency between organizations and the public regarding AI development and use

Background

Even though generative AI have been around since the 1950s, a major step forward was taken by OpenAI, who scaled generative AI models using the Internet and created the GPT text models we know today. Along with ChatGPT, OpenAI also created DALL-E, which generated original artworks based on user prompts. With this, the AI craze began and companies rushed to create products such as Stability AI and LensaAI. Soon, it is apparent that generative AI is the "next greatest thing" as it made its mark in the world. As stated by Ackerman, with generative AI becoming more dominant in text, image, and content generation, there have been concerns about the impact of generative AI on human creativity. In fact, many of these companies like Stability AI was sued for using the images of different artists without their permission [2]. Similar issues were faced in the music industry, where AI-generated music often use the voice and instrumentals of existing artists and producers.

Ethical Dilemmas:
  • Generative AI allows people to easily copy art styles, making it difficult to distinguish between what is truly original content
  • With the influx of AI-generated content on the Internet, we might foresee a future where machines monopolizes creativity
  • If the image is generated by AI, can it really be considered creative?

With these dilemmas in mind, I would like you to consider the following reflection questions:

  • How can we use generative AI to elevate human creavitity, rather than imitate it?
  • Do you think content created by AI should be considered art? What about the users behind them, are they artists? What really defines original and creative content?
  • Should copyright and intellectual property laws apply to content created by generative AI?

As society draws closer to a future where AI becomes a part of our daily lives, it is important to find ethical and sustainable ways in which humans use AI as tools to scale our work efficiency.
